Direct Air Capture (DAC) Technology Project: Computational Simulations of Microchannels

OBJECTIVE: Direct air capture technology could be impactful for mitigate carbon dioxide and climate change. The main purpose of this project is to develop a cost-efficient method for capture carbon dioxide from the atmosphere.

TECHNICAL DETAILS: I have conducted research on carbon capture, storage, and utilization technologies to design low-cost direct air capture technology under the supervision of Professor T. Alan Hatton and Sahag Voskian PhD. I investigated operating condition effects on the captured carbon dioxide concentration and optimized conditions such as inlet gas flow rate, temperature, and pressure. I drafted microchannels computational-aided design (CAD) models using SolidWorks software program and devised computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations including chemical reaction, mass transport, and laminar channel flow using COMSOL Multiphysics software program.

RESULTS: Inlet pressure, temperature, and flow rate of the gas stream are optimized to increase the carbon dioxide capture efficiency. Capture efficiency increased by 20 percent for new operating conditions.

Electrochemically Mediated Amine Regeneration (EMAR) Project: Electrochemical Flow System Optimization

OBJECTIVE: The aim of the EMAR project is to design electrochemical-based processes for carbon capture systems and optimize the electrochemical flow system to enhance carbon dioxide removal efficiency.

TECHNICAL DETAILS: The capture system consists of sealed electrochemical flow system and flow patterns shaped with baffle structures. Baffles are regulating the transport of species and heat transfer through the electrochemical cell and manufactured using the laser cutting method. Pressure drop value decreased by changing geometric design parameters of the baffle structure, such as baffle length, baffle thickness, baffle number, and baffle angle. These configurations modeled using computer-aided design (CAD) software (SolidWorks) and simulated using computational fluid dynamics (CFD) software program (ANSYS-Fluent).

RESULTS: Developed electrochemical flow system configuration decreased pressure drop value by 60 percent. This project was funded by the Department of Energy (DOE) and Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT).

Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) Project: Design, Analysis, Test and Manufacturing

OBJECTIVE: Design, analyze, test, and manufacture an unmanned aerial vehicle capable of carrying the payload to designated coordinates for military & civil operations.

TECHNICAL DETAILS: On the theoretical side of the project, conducted research on unmanned aerial vehicle design under the supervision of Professor Yahya Dogu and drafted conceptual design. Detailed design completed after several prototype testing. Aerodynamic properties improved using commercial simulation software program (ANSYS Fluent) and devised computational fluid dynamics simulations to determine aerodynamically efficient fuselage geometry.

On the experimental side of the project, I performed subsonic wind tunnel experiments of the aerial vehicle and validated computational predictions using test results. I crafted a tensile testing sample to ensure that wing material is durable under extreme weather conditions. I developed a custom 3d printer for use in the manufacturing process of the vehicle and updated software to improve printing quality. I manufactured wing components using composite manufacturing processes. Flight and autonomous system tests operated for the payload delivery mission.

RESULTS: Participated in International Unmanned Aerial Vehicle Competition and received honorable mention award. Led the aerodynamics and mechanical design team of 7 members and operated an unmanned aerial vehicle as a UAV pilot. The project was funded by Scientific and Research Council of Turkey (Equivalent to National Science Foundation), and Republic of Turkey Ministry of Industry and Technology

Aircraft Engine Secondary Air System Optimization Project: Computational Simulations of Sealing Components

OBJECTIVE: Secondary air system design has a crucial role in aircraft engine cooling and oiling. The main objective of this project is to develop low-cost sealing components and reduce leakage flow for the secondary air system.

TECHNICAL DETAILS: Labyrinth seals and honeycomb seals widely use in the propulsion systems such as turbofan engines. I redesigned sealing components specifically labyrinth and honeycomb seals and optimized geometric design parameters such as stator-rotor clearance and sealing component angle.

RESULTS: Sealing performance enhanced and leakage flow reduced by 55 percent. The project was published and presented at the National Aerospace and Space Conference.

Turbofan Engine Project: On-Design Parametric Analysis

OBJECTIVE: The main concern is specific fuel consumption and thrust-to-weight ratio for military turbofan engines. The project aimed to optimize performance parameters such as bypass ratio, turbine exit temperature, and engine diameter for engine performance optimization.

TECHNICAL DETAILS: Preliminary research conducted for the on-design analysis of gas turbine engines and determined main performance parameters for enhancing the overall performance. I carried out a parametric design analysis for reference military turbofan engine (EJ200) using GasTurb software. I optimized performance parameters to reduce specific fuel consumption and increase the thrust-to-weight ratio using analysis results.

RESULTS: Reference military turbofan engine was optimized using on-design parametric analysis to reach high performance and durability in flight. As a result, the specific fuel consumption was reduced by 25 percent, and the thrust-to-weight ratio was enhanced by 20 percent.

Aerodynamic Inverse Design Project: Airfoil Optimization

OBJECTIVE: The airfoil generates lift force by exerting a downward force on the air as it flows past. The main purpose of this project is to generate more lift force and reduce drag force on airfoil shape as well.

TECHNICAL DETAILS: The lift-to-drag ratio is critical parameter for aerodynamic performance. I conducted research on aerodynamic analysis tools and determined the design methodology. I drafted a new airfoil shape using the XFoil program and implemented the full-inverse design method to increase the lift coefficient of the airfoil. I redesigned a new airfoil and compared lift, drag, and moment coefficients with reference airfoil.

RESULTS: Reference airfoil was optimized and redesigned using aerodynamic inverse design methodologies. The lift-to-drag ratio was increased by 16.85 percent and the stall angle increased for the new airfoil geometry.
